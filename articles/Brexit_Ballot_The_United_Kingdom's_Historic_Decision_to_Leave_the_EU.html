Brexit Ballot: The United Kingdom's Historic Decision to Leave the EU



1/17/2023
THIBAUDON Arthur



The results of the Brexit referendum, as reported by the UK Electoral Commission, show the voting outcomes by region:


1. East: Remain - 1,448,616, Leave - 1,880,367

2. East Midlands: Remain - 1,033,036, Leave - 1,475,479

3. London: Remain - 2,263,519, Leave - 1,513,232

4. North East: Remain - 562,595, Leave - 778,103

5. North West: Remain - 1,699,020, Leave - 1,966,925.

6. Northern Ireland: Remain - 440,707, Leave - 349,442

7. Scotland: Remain - 1,661,191, Leave - 1,018,322

8. South East: Remain - 2,391,718, Leave - 2,567,965

9. South West: Remain - 1,503,019, Leave - 1,669,711

10. Wales: Remain - 772,347, Leave - 854,572

11. West Midlands: Remain - 1,207,175, Leave - 1,755,687

12. Yorkshire and the Humber: Remain - 1,158,298, Leave - 1,580,937


These figures reflect the final count of valid votes, with variations in turnout and electorate size across different regions.


1. Regional Differences: 

   

- London: A strong majority for "Remain" is noticeable in London. This reflects the metropolitan and diverse nature of the city, which often has different political and economic perspectives compared to other parts of the UK.

   

- Scotland and Northern Ireland: Both regions also show a majority for "Remain." Scotland's vote is particularly significant given its historical and ongoing discussions about independence from the UK. Northern Ireland's result reflects concerns about the impact of Brexit on the Irish border and the Good Friday Agreement.

   

- England and Wales: Outside of London, many regions in England and Wales show a majority for "Leave." This includes the North East, North West, East and West Midlands, South East, South West, and Wales. These results indicate a more pronounced Eurosceptic sentiment, possibly driven by concerns over sovereignty, immigration, and economic factors.


2. Urban vs. Rural Divide: 

   - The map may hint at an urban-rural divide, with more urbanized areas like London, Manchester, and Liverpool (within the North West region) leaning towards "Remain" while more rural areas tend to favor "Leave."


3. Economic Factors:

   

- Economic disparities could be a factor. Regions that have historically faced economic challenges or felt left behind by globalization and economic policies might have seen Brexit as an opportunity for change.


4. Cultural and Identity Factors:

   

- Cultural and identity issues are also likely at play. Regions with a strong sense of local identity might view the EU as a threat to their cultural autonomy.


5. Percentage vs. Absolute Numbers:

   

- Viewing the results in terms of percentages versus absolute numbers provides different perspectives. For example, while London had a strong percentage in favor of "Remain," the high population means this translates into a significant number of votes. Similarly, the cumulative effect of smaller percentages in high-population regions can impact the overall result.


6. Demographic Factors:

   

- Demographics likely played a role. Younger, more diverse, and highly educated populations, typically found in urban centers, tended to favor "Remain," while older and less urban populations leaned towards "Leave."


7. Implications for UK Politics:

   

- These results reflect deep divisions within the UK, both geographically and demographically. The varying attitudes towards the EU across regions pose challenges for political representation and policy-making.


In summary, the Brexit referendum map underscores the complex interplay of economic, cultural, demographic, and political factors that influenced the UK's decision to leave the EU. This complexity highlights the challenges in addressing regional disparities and national unity in the post-Brexit era.
